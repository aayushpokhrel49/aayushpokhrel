Why People Are Leaving Nepal and Settling Abroad: A Deep Look into the Modern Migration Trend

Aayush Pokhrel
June 26, 2025
10 min read

In recent years, the phenomenon of people leaving Nepal and settling abroad is a multifaceted issue driven by a combination of economic, social, educational, and personal factors. Over the years, international migration from Nepal has grown significantly, with many individuals seeking better opportunities beyond their homeland.

1. Economic Factors: The Primary Driver

Poverty, unemployment, and underemployment are among the most significant push factors prompting Nepalis to migrate. Despite some economic progress, Nepal continues to grapple with high youth unemployment and limited job opportunities, especially in the formal sector. Low wages and poor working conditions further discourage young people from staying. Many opt to work abroad where they can earn higher incomes, improve their living standards, and support their families back home through remittances. In fact, remittances have become a cornerstone of Nepal's economy, contributing significantly to GDP and household incomes.

  • Low Salary Structure
  • Minimal industrial development
  • Scarce Private Industry jobs

2. Quality of Life & Basic Services

For many, the decision to settle abroad is driven by the desire for a better life not just income, but access to quality healthcare, public services, and law enforcement.

  • Weak healthcare system: Lack of hospitals, doctors, and medical facilities, especially in rural areas.
  • Unemployment: Few job opportunities and low wages push people to seek work abroad.
  • Poor infrastructure: Unreliable electricity, bad roads, and limited internet affect daily life and business.
  • Low-quality education: Schools and colleges often lack resources and trained teachers.

In contrast, Nepal continues to struggle with inconsistent infrastructure, political instability, and corruption all of which push people toward more stable nations.

3. Education and Career Prospects

Nepal's education system, while improving, still lacks the quality and global recognition that many students seek. As a result, an increasing number of young Nepalis are migrating for higher education and better career prospects abroad. This trend is particularly noticeable among those pursuing technical and professional degrees such as medicine, engineering, and management. Once these students complete their education, many choose to stay in their host countries to find employment, leading to a brain drain for Nepal.

"After studying engineering for 4 years, I earned less than a call center job abroad. It made no sense to stay."

– A Nepalese Student from Australia

4. Personal and Social Motivations

Beyond economic and educational reasons, personal motivations also play a role in migration decisions. Marriage, family reunification, and personal aspirations are key non-economic drivers of migration. For instance, some women migrate after marrying someone residing abroad, while others move to join family members who have already settled overseas. Additionally, modern facilities, better infrastructure, and perceived quality of life in foreign countries attract many Nepalis to settle abroad permanently.

5. Political and Social Instability

Nepal's history of political instability, frequent changes in government, and slow policy implementation have created an environment of uncertainty. Issues like corruption, lack of rule of law, and weak governance further deter investment and development, pushing citizens to look elsewhere for stability and security. Moreover, the uneven distribution of resources across different regions of Nepal has resulted in localized deprivation, encouraging migration from rural to urban areas and eventually abroad.

6. Cultural and Psychological Dimensions

Migration from Nepal is not about economics it is also shaped by cultural values and societal expectations. There is a growing perception among youth that success and prestige are tied to living or working abroad. This "migration culture" is reinforced by stories of relatives or community members who have found financial success overseas, creating a cycle of aspiration and emulation. However, this often leads to emotional challenges, including loneliness and separation from family, especially for elderly parents left behind.

7. Environmental and Geographical Factors

Nepal's rugged terrain and vulnerability to natural disasters such as earthquakes and landslides also influence migration patterns. People living in remote or disaster-prone areas may find it difficult to sustain livelihoods, prompting them to relocate to safer and more prosperous regions outside the country.

Personal Stories from Nepali Migrants

To understand the mindset of today's Nepali youth, we reached out to several individuals who have settled or worked abroad. Their responses reflect deep frustration and a strong desire for dignity and opportunity.

"I had a master's degree, but the only job I got in Nepal paid Rs. 15,000. In Australia, I earned that in a few hours."

– A student turned PR holder in Melbourne

"I loved teaching in Nepal, but I couldn't feed my family. Now I clean offices in Dubai, but at least I can save something every month."

– Former school teacher, now migrant worker

"We waited years for government service exams. When the vacancy finally came, it was filled with connections. That's when I gave up and moved abroad."

– A frustrated PSC aspirant, now in Canada

"As a nurse, I always wanted to help people. But hospitals in Nepal are understaffed, underpaid, and unsafe. Working in the UK gave me dignity and respect."

– Registered nurse in Manchester

"Life abroad is not as easy as it looks on Instagram, but at least I see progress. Back home, we were just surviving."

– A nurse living in the UK

"My dream was to be a software engineer in Nepal. But startups don't pay enough. Now I'm working remotely for a US company from Portugal."

– IT graduate from Kathmandu

"People said I'd come back in a year. It's been 7 years. My kids now speak more English than Nepali."

– Nepali mother settled in USA

Brain Drain Concern

The mass migration of skilled professionals is creating a significant brain drain for Nepal, with sectors like healthcare and IT being particularly affected. This exodus of talent could have long-term consequences for the country's development.

Conclusion

In summary, the decision of Nepalis to leave their country and settle abroad is influenced by a complex interplay of economic hardship, limited educational and career opportunities, personal aspirations, political instability, and cultural trends. While migration brings benefits such as remittance inflows and exposure to global experiences, it also poses challenges like brain drain and social fragmentation. Addressing the root causes will require comprehensive policy interventions, improved governance, and sustained investment in human capital development to make Nepal a more attractive place to live and work.
